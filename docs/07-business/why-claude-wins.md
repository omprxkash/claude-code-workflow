## The paradigm shift: drag-and-drop → natural language

Old automation (n8n, Zapier, Make): You spend weeks learning a platform, building flows with drag-and-drop nodes, connecting modules visually. Change one thing and you rebuild the diagram.

New automation (Claude Code): You write bullet points.

```
Old way — an n8n flow with 20+ nodes, each one
hand-configured, learning the platform took months.

New way — the same workflow in Claude Code:
• When someone replies to a cold outreach campaign, receive a webhook
• Look up the campaign in a Google Sheet to find talking points and example replies
• Research the person who replied
• Generate a short, friendly reply
• Skip if they said "unsubscribe" or "remove me"
• Skip if there's no knowledge base for this campaign
• Otherwise, send the reply automatically
```

Anyone on a team can read, edit, and extend the bullet-point version. The n8n version requires a specialist. And the Claude version can be changed in seconds — add a step by writing a sentence.

This isn't iterative improvement on automation tools. It's a different category.

Vulcan builds software that runs inside government agencies. Three founders — two of them couldn't write a single line of code. One hadn't touched code since a high school JavaScript class.

## What Claude Code actually does differently

Four things:

1. **It does the work.** Describe what you want in plain English — a full app, a website, an automation. Minutes, not months.
2. **It's agentic.** It takes action towards a goal instead of answering questions. It builds, then tests, then finds the bug, then fixes it. A chatbot leaves the in-between work to you.
3. **It runs in parallel.** Multiple Claude agents can run simultaneously, including while you sleep.
4. **It remembers.** It knows your business, your priorities, your past decisions. At scale it becomes a more reliable record of your work than your own memory.

When those four stack together, the gap between "idea in a notes app" and "working product" collapses.

## Horizontal leverage — why "automating one role" is the wrong frame

When people think about AI replacing work, they think: automate 100% of one role. That's a linear trade.

The real opportunity is different: automate 90% of 10,000 roles.

```
100% of 1 role  = 1 unit of economic value
90% of 10,000 roles = 9,000 units of economic value
```

No AI system today fully replaces a role end-to-end — there are always gaps where human judgment is needed. But removing 90% of the execution overhead from every knowledge worker in an organization is a much larger opportunity than eliminating any one job entirely.

The industrial revolution parallel: a loom didn't eliminate seamstresses — it changed what they did. One person used to produce 10 garments per day by hand. After the loom, they supervised machines producing 10,000 per day. The human moved from execution to oversight and direction.

Agentic workflows do the same thing for knowledge work. The question isn't "which role gets replaced?" It's "which parts of every role can be handed off, so humans spend more time on the 10% that requires actual judgment?"

## How to actually start: four steps

**Step 1 — Get on a paid plan**

$20/month (Pro) is the entry point for Claude Code. Not another subscription — the cheapest employee you'll ever hire.

**Step 2 — Pick one real task**

Not 10. One. Something you keep putting off, something repetitive, something you'd normally outsource. Organizing receipts, a weekly report, sorting emails, an idea from your notes app. That's your first project.

**Step 3 — Describe it clearly, then challenge it**

Tell Claude what the task is, how you do it today, and what a win looks like. Before it builds anything, make it argue with you. Make it play devil's advocate. These models are sycophantic by default — they want to hype your idea up and start building, even if there are holes. Force it to look at everything from every angle first.

See [four-upgrades.md](../06-workflows/four-upgrades.md) — the `/roast` skill does this automatically.

**Step 4 — Build and verify**

Start with the smallest version that actually works. When it tells you it's done, don't take its word. Make it prove it's done. Have it run the thing on a real example and show you the output. It can control a browser — anything you'd do to verify manually, the AI can do.

Your first version will probably be 60–70% right. That's normal. Treat the misses as feedback. Tell it what was wrong in plain English and let it fix its own work.
